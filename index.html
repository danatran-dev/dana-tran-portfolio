An internal AI tool built for Google's sales org that automated campaign creation. Reps input the campaign's purpose, landing page, and KPIs — the agent returned copy-and-paste-ready creative assets tailored to campaign type. The product was scoped, built, and adopted within a single quarter.

Sales reps were losing hours per launch to manual campaign creation — researching text variants, audience logic, and creative direction from scratch for every advertiser. The problem wasn't capability — it was speed and repeatability.

Led internal launch — wrote the onboarding guide, ran enablement sessions, and built a feedback loop to capture edge cases and ship iterative improvements.

Ran structured discovery sessions with reps across SMB and enterprise segments. The critical insight: reps were often receiving vague advertiser briefs and needed the product to handle ambiguity — not just structured inputs. This shaped the core product requirement: graceful handling of incomplete context. Rep feedback post-launch directly drove the first two iterations.

Metis is an agent post-training infrastructure company helping enterprises take AI agents from prototype to production. The product was technically differentiated but entering a market where the category had no established name — buyers knew their agents were failing in production but had no language for the solution they needed.

The core challenge was a PMF problem: a powerful product with no clear ICP, no defined use case hierarchy, and a sales motion targeting everyone and converting no one. Without a sharp answer to "who is this for and why now," the product couldn't build the pipeline it needed to validate at launch.

Ran a full product discovery process — qualitative buyer interviews, ICP audit, and competitive landscape analysis. Rebuilt the ICP from scratch around enterprises with agents already in production, where reliability failures had direct revenue consequences. Defined the JTBD framework, rewrote the product narrative from "evaluation tooling" to post-training infrastructure, and built a full GTM blueprint covering positioning, success metrics, and a three-tiered outreach framework mapped to distinct buyer pain patterns.

Discovery calls with potential buyers revealed the exact language buyers used to describe the problem: their agents were "hitting a ceiling" — working in demos but breaking in production. That verbatim language became the anchor for repositioning. Interviews also surfaced a critical product insight: prototype-stage companies were structurally unable to buy, not just unready. Removing them from the ICP was a product decision, not just a sales one.

Hook'd is a fashion and culture publication built from scratch at UT Austin. Before it was a brand, it was a product insight: the existing media landscape created a structural barrier for students — you needed a portfolio to get published, but you needed publishing credits to build a portfolio. Hook'd was designed to eliminate that barrier entirely.

The problem wasn't content quality or audience size — it was access architecture. Every major publication in the space was optimized for established creatives, which meant the students who most needed a platform were systematically excluded. The product needed to be built around a completely different value proposition: participation over prestige.

Defined the core product principle as "the publication where you build the portfolio, not the one that requires it" — and designed every feature of the experience around that thesis. Contributor onboarding, editorial support, production timelines, and partnership strategy were all built to lower the barrier to participation and maximize the career signal for contributors. Secured partnerships with 10+ beauty brands and 24 industry professionals to create a self-reinforcing growth loop where contributor outcomes became the product's most powerful proof point.

The clearest validation came from how contributors talked about Hook'd unprompted — in job interviews, on LinkedIn, in conversations with recruiters. They didn't say "I write for a student magazine." They said "I was on the editorial team at Hook'd" and listed it as a professional credential. That behavioral signal proved the product was delivering on its core promise and reshaped how the brand communicated externally — centering real user outcomes as the proof, not the publication itself.

Plaza Aesthetics is a medical spa in a highly competitive, appointment-driven market where client trust, experience quality, and post-visit follow-through are the primary drivers of both retention and word-of-mouth growth. When joining the operation, there was no existing marketing infrastructure — no paid acquisition strategy, no automated client communication, and no structured retention program. Everything needed to be built from zero.

The business was operating without a repeatable system for attracting new clients or keeping existing ones. New customer acquisition relied entirely on walk-ins and organic referrals with no paid amplification. On the retention side, clients who had positive experiences were not being re-engaged after their visit — no follow-up cadence, no appointment reminders, and no mechanism to turn satisfied clients into active referrers.

Audited the full customer journey from first touchpoint to post-visit to map where drop-off was occurring. Identified two distinct product gaps: no acquisition system and no retention infrastructure. Built both from scratch — paid acquisition across Google and Meta, and a post-visit workflow covering automated follow-up, appointment reminders, and a referral program. Treated scheduling and payment systems as product surfaces, not just operational tools, and redesigned both for throughput and user experience.

Spent time observing the in-person experience and speaking directly with staff and returning clients to understand what drove people to come back — and what caused them not to. The clearest finding: clients who felt remembered returned. Those who received no communication after their visit didn't. This user insight anchored the entire retention strategy around relationship over transaction — shaping the tone, timing, and content of every automated touchpoint in the post-visit workflow.

45% new client acquisition growth, 38% retention increase, 50% reduction in wait time — built in under six months with no existing infrastructure. The project demonstrated that customer experience is a product problem, and that the highest-leverage interventions are often in the systems behind the experience, not the experience itself. When you understand why users leave, you can engineer the conditions that make them stay.
